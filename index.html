LCM and its applications

Least Common Multiple (LCM):

The Least Common Multiple (LCM) of two or more numbers is the smallest number that is a multiple of each of the numbers. In other words, the LCM is the smallest number that can be divided by all the given numbers without leaving a remainder.

For example:

The multiples of 6 are 6, 12, 18, 24, 30, …
The multiples of 8 are 8, 16, 24, 32, …

The smallest number common in both lists is 24.
Thus the LCM of 6 and 8 is 24.

How to Find the LCM:

1. Listing Multiples Method:

  • Write the multiples of each number.
  • Identify the smallest number common to all lists.

For example:

The multiples of 4 are 4, 8, 12, 16, 20, 24, 28, 32, …
The multiples of 5 are 5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30 …

The smallest number common in both lists is 20. So, the LCM of 4 and 5 is 20.

2. Prime Factorisation Method:

  • Write the prime factorization of each number.
  • Take the highest powers of all prime factors.
  • Multiply them to get the LCM.

For Example: Find the LCM of 12 and 18.

Prime factorisation of 12 is 2 × 2 × 3 = 22 × 3
Prime factorisation of 18 is 2 × 3 × 3 = 2 × 32

Take the highest power of all prime factors: 22 × 32 = 36
Hence, the LCM of 12 and 18 is 36.

3. Division Method:

  • Write the numbers in a row.
  • Divide them by a common prime factor, repeating until no common factor exists.
  • Multiply all divisors to get the LCM.

Multiplying all the factors : 2 × 2 × 2 × 3 × 5 = 120.
So, the LCM of 8, 12 and 20 is 120.

LCM Applications:

The Least Common Multiple (LCM) is used in real-life scenarios where we need to find a common period or shared event for two or more repeating cycles. It is helpful in scheduling, organizing items, and solving problems involving synchronized events. LCM is widely applied in problems related to time intervals, arranging items into groups, and ensuring uniformity in distribution.

Example: LCM in Scheduling Events

Problem: Two buses leave a station at the same time. One bus arrives at the station every 12 minutes, and the other every 15 minutes. After how many minutes will both buses arrive at the station together?

Solution: To find the time when both buses will arrive at the station together, we calculate the LCM of 12 and 15.
12 = 2 × 2 × 3, 15 = 3 × 5 LCM of 12 and 15 = 2 × 2 × 3 × 5 = 60

Both buses will arrive at the station together after 60 minutes.
